You have two seconds. Maybe less.

In a world where the average human attention span hovers around eight seconds—down from twelve just two decades ago—the first moment of your content is everything. Scroll past. Swipe away. Click "Not Interested." The platforms have optimized for frictionless rejection, and if your opening doesn't grab the viewer by the collar, you vanish into the algorithmic void.

That first moment is called the hook. And mastering it is the difference between 200 views and 2 million.

This isn't about clickbait. It's about understanding the psychology of attention, the mechanics of each platform, and the specific hook structures that have been proven to stop thumbs mid-scroll. Whether you're creating for TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube, or LinkedIn, the hook is the single highest-leverage element in your content.

Let's break down what actually works.

## The Psychology Behind the Scroll-Stopper

Every hook works because it triggers a specific psychological response. Understanding these triggers gives you the ability to engineer attention rather than hope for it.

**The Curiosity Gap.** This is the most powerful tool in a creator's arsenal. You present a piece of information that creates an open loop—something the brain desperately wants to close. "The one setting on your phone that's draining your battery 3x faster" works because the viewer needs to know what that setting is. Their brain experiences mild discomfort until the loop closes. The key is that the payoff must deliver. If you tease a mystery and the answer is obvious, you've burned trust.

**Loss Aversion.** Humans are wired to avoid loss more strongly than they seek gain. "Stop making this mistake" or "The SEO error that's costing you 60% of your traffic" taps into the fear that you're currently losing something you didn't even know you had. It's negative framing, but it's brutally effective because it creates urgency.

**Social Identity.** "Unpopular opinion: your thumbnail is fine, your hook is the problem" signals that the creator belongs to an insider group (people who actually understand content strategy) and invites the viewer to join. It flatters the viewer by implying they're smart enough to handle a contrarian take.

**Pattern Interrupt.** The brain craves predictability because it saves energy. When something breaks the expected pattern, your brain snaps to attention. This is why sudden loud noises, unexpected cuts, or counter-intuitive statements work. "I tried posting every day for a month. Here's what *actually* happened" breaks the pattern of vague, generic advice.

## The Four Hook Types That Dominate Viral Content

After analyzing thousands of viral posts across TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, and LinkedIn, four hook structures consistently outperform everything else.

### 1. The Negative Hook

The negative hook is simple: tell the audience what *not* to do, or warn them about a mistake they're probably making.

Examples:
- "Stop posting at 9 AM. Here's why."
- "The #1 reason your Reels aren't hitting the For You page."
- "Don't buy a ring light until you watch this."

Why it works: Negativity bias is real. Our brains process negative information faster and remember it longer. A negative hook also positions you as an authority—you know enough to warn people, which implies expertise. The viewer feels you're looking out for them, which builds trust rapidly.

**Where it works best:** YouTube and LinkedIn. Both platforms reward educational, authority-driven content. On YouTube, negative hooks in the first 15 seconds significantly improve retention. On LinkedIn, negative hooks generate 40% more comments because professionals love to weigh in with their own takes.

**The risk:** Overdo it and you become the "doom and gloom" creator. Balance negative hooks with positive, aspirational content.

### 2. The Cliffhanger Hook

A cliffhanger promises a reveal. The viewer must watch to the end to get the answer.

Examples:
- "I tested 5 AI hook generators so you don't have to. The results were shocking."
- "This one change doubled my engagement in 7 days."
- "The algorithm change nobody is talking about."

Why it works: It's the purest form of the curiosity gap. You're making a specific promise that can only be fulfilled by consuming the entire piece of content. The key is specificity. "This one change" is better than "some changes." "Doubled my engagement in 7 days" is better than "improved my engagement."

**Where it works best:** TikTok and Instagram Reels. These platforms are built for short-form, high-retention loops. A cliffhanger hook at the start combined with a satisfying payoff at the end creates the "rewatch" signal that platforms love. If viewers watch your Reel multiple times, the algorithm rewards you heavily.

**The risk:** If the payoff doesn't match the promise, you destroy trust. Never make the reveal underwhelming. If you promise a "shocking" result, it had better be shocking.

### 3. The Unpopular Opinion Hook

This hook states a contrarian position and invites debate.

Examples:
- "Unpopular opinion: you don't need a $2000 camera to go viral."
- "Everyone is wrong about posting frequency."
- "Your engagement rate doesn't matter. Here's what does."

Why it works: Humans are argumentative by nature. When you state an opinion that challenges conventional wisdom, the viewer's brain immediately starts formulating a counter-argument—which means they have to watch your content to see if you address it. You've created engagement before they've even finished the first sentence.

**Where it works best:** LinkedIn and Twitter/X. These platforms are debate-friendly. The comment section is the goal. On LinkedIn, an unpopular opinion post that generates 100 comments will be shown to thousands more people than a post that generates 10 likes. Controversy (within reason) is a distribution strategy.

**The risk:** Make sure your opinion is defensible. If you're wrong, the comments will eat you alive. Also, avoid truly divisive topics like politics or religion unless that's explicitly your niche.

### 4. The Curiosity Gap Hook

This hook uses an intriguing statement or question to create an information void.

Examples:
- "The one SEO mistake costing you 60% of your traffic."
- "Why every creator is switching to this AI tool."
- "Here's what happens when you use the wrong background color in product photos."

Why it works: It's the most versatile hook type because it can be adapted to any niche, platform, or format. The curiosity gap exploits the brain's natural desire for completion. When you hear "the one thing," your brain wants to know what that thing is. When you hear "what happens when," your brain wants to see the result.

**Where it works best:** Everywhere. The curiosity gap is platform-agnostic. It works in YouTube titles, TikTok captions, Instagram thumbnails, blog headlines, and email subject lines. It's the Swiss Army knife of hooks.

**The risk:** Overuse leads to desensitization. If every piece of content promises "the one secret," viewers stop believing you. Vary your hook types to keep your content feeling fresh.

## How Hooks Work on Different Platforms

A hook that crushes on TikTok might flop on YouTube, and vice versa. Each platform has its own culture, viewing behavior, and algorithmic incentives. You need to tailor your hook to the platform.

### TikTok: Speed and Surprise

TikTok rewards the fastest possible hook. The first 1-2 seconds are critical. You need to show, not tell. Text overlays, sudden movements, and visual surprises work better than talking heads.

**Best hooks for TikTok:**
- Visual pattern interrupts (jump cut, zoom, unexpected object)
- Text-based curiosity gaps ("Wait for it..." with a text overlay)
- Immediate value proposition within the first frame

**Pro tip:** On TikTok, the hook needs to work *without sound*. A huge percentage of TikTok users watch on mute. Your hook must be visually communicated.

### Instagram Reels: Aesthetic and Relatable

Instagram users expect polish. The hook should match the visual aesthetic of your feed. Relatability outperforms shock value here.

**Best hooks for Instagram:**
- Relatable statements ("Tell me you're a creator without telling me you're a creator")
- Aesthetic curiosity (beautiful visuals with a text overlay asking a question)
- Transformation promises ("How my account went from 500 to 50K in 30 days")

**Pro tip:** Instagram's algorithm heavily weights saves and shares. A hook that makes someone want to save your post for later (e.g., a checklist or resource) will outperform one that just gets views.

### YouTube: Retention and Context

YouTube is the only platform where the hook doesn't need to happen in the first second. You have about 15-30 seconds because viewers are more intentional. However, the click-through rate (CTR) from the recommendation sidebar is determined almost entirely by the thumbnail and title—which function as the pre-hook.

**Best hooks for YouTube:**
- Story-driven openings ("It started like any other Tuesday...")
- Statistical bombshells ("97% of creators make this mistake")
- Personal stakes ("I almost quit creating content until I tried this")

**Pro tip:** The first 30 seconds of a YouTube video determine whether someone stays for the next 10 minutes. Use a "content loop" where you tease the most interesting part of the video in the first 15 seconds, then transition to the setup.

### LinkedIn: Authority and Insight

LinkedIn hooks need to feel professional but conversational. The goal is to stop a busy professional mid-scroll and make them think.

**Best hooks for LinkedIn:**
- Contrarian takes ("Hot take: your SEO strategy is backwards")
- Data-driven openings ("I analyzed 10,000 viral posts. Here's the one thing they all had in common")
- Personal vulnerability ("I failed for 6 months before I figured this out")

**Pro tip:** LinkedIn hooks that generate comments are gold. Ask a question or make a statement that invites discussion. The algorithm prioritizes engagement over views.

## A/B Testing Your Hooks

Even the best creators can't predict which hook will resonate. You need to test. Here's a simple system.

**For short-form video:** Create two versions of the same content with different hooks. Post Version A on TikTok and Version B on Reels (or use the cross-posting feature). After 24 hours, compare the first-second retention metrics. Whichever version retained more viewers in the first 3 seconds, use that hook structure for your next batch of content.

**For YouTube:** Test thumbnails and titles simultaneously. Upload the same video with different title/thumbnail combinations to a draft or use YouTube's built-in Test & Compare feature. Keep the video hook the same until you find a winning title-thumbnail combo.

**For blog posts:** Use your [SEO Analyzer](/seo-analyzer) to test which headlines generate the best click-through potential. The headline is your blog hook. Use data to inform your decisions.

**For email:** A/B test subject lines with your email list. Subject lines are hooks for opens. Measure open rates after 24 hours and let the data guide you.

The most viral content doesn't rely on a single hook type—it stacks them.

**Example hook stack for a YouTube video:**
- **Title:** "The 2-Second Secret to Going Viral" (Curiosity Gap)
- **Thumbnail:** Shocked face with text "97% FAIL" (Pattern Interrupt + Loss Aversion)
- **First 5 seconds:** "I analyzed 500 viral videos to find the one thing they all share. What I found changed how I create content forever." (Cliffhanger + Authority)
- **Next 10 seconds:** "And I'm going to show you exactly how to use it. But first, let me show you what most creators get wrong." (Unpopular Opinion + Value Promise)

Each layer reinforces the next. The viewer is pulled through the content by an escalating series of hooks.

## Common Hook Mistakes to Avoid

**Mistake #1: The bait and switch.** Your hook promises one thing, and your content delivers something else. This destroys trust and increases bounce rates. The algorithm notices when viewers leave quickly.

**Mistake #2: Being vague.** "Here's a tip that will help" is weak. "Here's the exact hook structure that got me 10 million views" is specific. Specificity creates credibility.

**Mistake #3: Forgetting the platform.** A hook that works on YouTube rarely works verbatim on TikTok. Adapt the format, length, and delivery to match platform norms.

**Mistake #4: The slow wind-up.** Don't build context before the hook. The hook IS the opening. Context comes after. Show the hook in the first frame. Everything else can wait.

**Mistake #5: Ignoring the visual hook.** On visual platforms, what you show matters as much as what you say. A cluttered background, bad lighting, or boring visuals can kill a good spoken hook.

Viral hooks aren't magic. They're patterns. They're psychological triggers applied to specific platforms with specific audience expectations. Anyone can learn them. Anyone can master them.

The difference between creators who go viral and creators who don't isn't luck—it's understanding that the first two seconds are more important than the next ten minutes. Because if you don't win those two seconds, there are no next ten minutes.

Title: 5 Sneaky SEO Mistakes That Are Killing Your Rankings (and How to Fix Them in 2026)
Excerpt: Common SEO mistakes that tank Google rankings: keyword stuffing in alt tags, mobile-first indexing issues, thin content, broken links, duplicate content, and slow page speed. Actionable fixes included.

```
You check your Google rankings every morning like clockwork. And every morning, the news gets worse. Position 5 becomes position 8. Position 8 disappears from the first page entirely. You didn't get penalized. You didn't get de-indexed. But your traffic is bleeding out slowly, and you can't figure out why.

Chances are, it's not one big mistake. It's a handful of sneaky, easy-to-overlook errors that are silently sabotaging your SEO. The kind of mistakes that don't trigger a manual penalty but slowly erode your authority, confuse Google's crawlers, and push your pages deeper into the SERPs.

Here are the five sneakiest SEO mistakes killing your rankings in 2026—and exactly how to fix each one.

## Mistake #1: Keyword Stuffing in Alt Tags

You know alt text is important for image SEO. You've been diligent about adding keywords to every image on your site. But here's the problem: you've been treating alt tags like keyword deposit boxes.

**What it looks like:**
```html
<img src="red-shoes.jpg" alt="red shoes buy red shoes online cheap red shoes running shoes red sneakers">
```

This is keyword stuffing, plain and simple. And Google has gotten very good at detecting it.

**Why it's killing your rankings:** Alt text was designed for accessibility—to describe images to visually impaired users using screen readers. When Google sees alt text that's an obvious keyword dump, it understands the intent. The search algorithm now treats stuffed alt text as a spam signal, not a ranking boost. It can actually harm your overall page authority.

**How to fix it:**
- Write alt text that describes the image naturally, as if you're explaining it to someone who can't see it.
- Use 5-15 words maximum.
- Include your keyword only if it fits naturally. "Red running shoes on a concrete path" is excellent. "Red shoes buy red shoes online" is spam.
- Every image gets unique alt text. Never reuse the same alt text across multiple images.

**Pro tip:** Run your site through an [SEO Analyzer](/seo-analyzer) to identify which images have stuffed or missing alt text. Fix them one by one, starting with your highest-traffic pages.

## Mistake #2: Neglecting Mobile-First Indexing

Google switched to mobile-first indexing years ago, but many site owners still treat it as an afterthought. Here's what that means in practice: Google primarily uses the mobile version of your page to determine rankings, even for desktop searches.

**The sneaky problem:** Your desktop site looks great. Your mobile site technically works—it loads, it's responsive. But the mobile experience is degraded in subtle ways that Google notices.

**Common mobile-first indexing mistakes:**
- Content hidden behind tabs or accordions that Google can't easily access.
- Images that are too large for mobile viewports, causing layout shifts.
- Font sizes below 16px, which trigger zooming and poor UX signals.
- Interstitials and popups that cover the content on mobile screens.
- Different navigation structures between desktop and mobile, confusing crawlers.

**How to fix it:**
- Use Google Search Console's Mobile Usability report. It will flag specific issues.
- Test your pages with the Mobile-Friendly Test tool.
- Ensure all content visible on desktop is also accessible on mobile—no hidden text, no content blocked by JavaScript.
- Use responsive images with srcset attributes so the correct size loads for each device.
- Keep font sizes at 16px or above on mobile.
- Minimize popups on mobile. If you use them, make sure they're easy to dismiss.

**The 2026 reality:** Google now uses Core Web Vitals as a direct ranking factor, and mobile performance is the biggest driver of poor scores. If your Largest Contentful Paint (LCP) is above 2.5 seconds on mobile, you're losing rankings. If your First Input Delay (FID) is above 100ms, same story.

## Mistake #3: Thin Content That Violates E-E-A-T

E-E-A-T stands for Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness. It's not a direct ranking factor—it's a framework Google's quality raters use to evaluate content quality. But the algorithms have been trained to mirror rater judgments. If your content lacks E-E-A-T signals, you're fighting an uphill battle.

**The sneaky problem:** You think 300 words is "good enough." You think a generic article written by a freelance generalist with no byline is fine. You think you can skip adding author bios, citations, or original research.

You're wrong.

**Signs of thin content:**
- Pages under 500 words that don't fully answer the search query (with exceptions for very specific queries).
- Content that restates what's already on the first page of Google without adding anything new.
- Pages with no author attribution, or with authors who have no visible expertise in the topic.
- Content that lacks any original insight, data, or personal experience.
- Affiliate-heavy pages with minimal helpful content.

**How to fix it:**
- Expand thin pages to at least 800-1000 words for competitive topics, 1200-1500 for in-depth queries.
- Add author bios with links to their professional profiles or portfolios.
- Include original data, screenshots, examples from personal experience, or case studies.
- Cite authoritative sources when making factual claims.
- Remove or consolidate pages that don't add value.

**The E-E-A-T checklist for every page:**
1. Does the author have demonstrated expertise in this topic?
2. Does the content reflect first-hand experience or original research?
3. Is the information accurate, up-to-date, and sourced?
4. Is the website trustworthy (secure, transparent about ownership, clear about affiliate relationships)?

If you answer "no" to any of these, your content is underperforming its potential.

## Mistake #4: Broken Internal Links Causing Crawl Issues

You check your external links. You've fixed every 404 that points to external sites. But your internal links? You haven't looked at them in months.

**The sneaky problem:** When Google crawls your site, it follows internal links to discover new pages and understand your site structure. Every broken internal link is a dead end. It wastes crawl budget, confuses the link graph, and can prevent important pages from being indexed.

**Why it's worse than you think:**
- A broken internal link from a high-authority page to a cornerstone content page can dilute the flow of link equity.
- Google's crawlers will stop crawling deeper into your site if they encounter too many dead ends.
- Internal 404s create a poor user experience—when visitors click a link and get a dead page, they leave.

**Common causes of broken internal links:**
- Deleted or renamed pages without proper 301 redirects.
- URL structure changes after a site migration or redesign.
- Outdated navigation links pointing to archived content.
- Category or tag pages that were removed.
- Misspelled URLs in manually added links.

**How to fix it:**
- Use an [SEO analyzer](/seo-analyzer) to crawl your site and identify all broken internal links.
- Set up 301 redirects from dead pages to relevant live pages.
- Use a "soft 404" checker to find pages that return 200 status codes but are actually empty or error pages.
- Implement a custom 404 page that helps users find what they need.
- Run a crawl monthly to catch new broken links before they accumulate.

**Pro tip:** Pay special attention to your navigation and footer links. These are crawled most frequently, and a broken link here wastes more crawl budget than a broken link deep in your archives.

## Mistake #5: Duplicate Content Penalties (Yes, They're Real)

Google says duplicate content doesn't result in a penalty. What Google actually means is that it doesn't result in a manual penalty. Algorithmically, duplicate content absolutely harms your rankings.

**The sneaky problem:** You're not copying content from other sites. But you might be creating internal duplicate content without realizing it.

**Common internal duplicate content issues:**
- WWW vs. non-WWW versions of your site both being accessible.
- HTTP vs. HTTPS versions both being accessible.
- Trailing slash vs. non-trailing slash versions of URLs.
- Session IDs or tracking parameters creating multiple URLs for the same page.
- Pagination creating near-identical pages (/page/1/, /page/2/, /page/3/).
- Print-friendly versions of pages accessible at separate URLs.
- Product variations creating similar but not identical pages.

**How to fix it:**
- Choose a canonical domain (www or non-www) and 301 redirect the other one.
- Force HTTPS and redirect all HTTP traffic.
- Be consistent with trailing slashes and use 301 redirects to enforce your preference.
- Use the rel="canonical" tag to tell Google which version of a page is the primary one.
- Set up parameter handling in Google Search Console.
- Add noindex tags to pagination pages beyond page 1.
- Use self-referencing canonicals on every page.

**The canonical tag is not a directive, it's a hint.** If Google sees conflicting signals (multiple canonicals, or a canonical pointing to a different page than your internal links suggest), Google may ignore your canonical and pick what it considers the best version. Make sure your canonicals align with your internal linking structure.

## Bonus Mistake: Slow Page Speed (The Silent Killer)

This one deserves its own section because it's the most common and most damaging mistake that gets ignored.

**The numbers don't lie:**
- 53% of mobile users abandon a page that takes longer than 3 seconds to load.
- A 1-second delay in page load time reduces conversions by 7%.
- Page speed is a direct ranking factor for both desktop and mobile.

**But here's the sneaky part:** You run your URL through Google PageSpeed Insights and it scores 85. That's good, right? Wrong. The score is less important than the actual metrics. You can have a score of 85 but a LCP of 3.5 seconds, and Google will rank the page that has a score of 72 but a LCP of 1.8 seconds higher every time.

**What to actually optimize:**
- **LCP (Largest Contentful Paint):** Optimize images, preload key resources, and use a CDN. Target under 2.5 seconds.
- **FID (First Input Delay):** Minimize JavaScript execution time. Target under 100ms.
- **CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift):** Set explicit dimensions on all images and embeds. Target under 0.1.

**How to fix it:**
- Compress all images using modern formats (WebP, AVIF).
- Implement lazy loading for below-the-fold images.
- Remove unused CSS and JavaScript.
- Use a content delivery network (CDN).
- Enable browser caching.
- Minimize render-blocking resources.
- Consider switching to a faster hosting provider if your TTFB (Time to First Byte) is consistently above 600ms.

## Putting It All Together: Your SEO Audit Checklist

Here's a step-by-step plan to identify and fix these sneaky mistakes:

**Week 1: Technical Foundation**
- Fix duplicate content issues (canonicals, redirects, parameter handling).
- Ensure HTTPS is enforced and www/non-www is canonicalized.

**Week 2: Mobile Audit**
- Run Google's Mobile-Friendly Test on your top 20 pages.
- Fix layout issues, font sizes, and tap target spacing.
- Check Core Web Vitals in Search Console.

**Week 3: Content Audit**
- Identify pages under 500 words that need expansion.
- Add author bios and E-E-A-T signals.
- Consolidate or remove thin content.

**Week 4: Link Audit**
- Crawl your site to find all broken internal links.
- Set up 301 redirects and fix navigation.
- Use your [SEO analyzer](/seo-analyzer) to monitor link health going forward.

**Ongoing:**
- Run a monthly site crawl.
- Monitor Core Web Vitals weekly.
- Update content quarterly to keep it fresh and accurate.

SEO isn't a set-it-and-forget-it game. The sites that win in 2026 are the ones that consistently audit, fix, and improve. These five mistakes are easy to make and easy to ignore—but they're also easy to fix once you know what to look for.

Start with one. Fix it today. Watch your rankings recover.

## The Rule: What Amazon and eBay Actually Require

### Amazon's Image Requirements

Amazon is the strictest marketplace when it comes to product photography. Here are the exact requirements for your main image:

- **Background must be pure white** (RGB 255, 255, 255). Not off-white. Not light gray. Not cream. Pure white.

- **No text, logos, watermarks, or graphics** on the main image. That's what secondary images are for.
- **No props, accessories, or other items** that aren't part of the product being sold.

- **Images must be at least 1000 pixels** on the longest side to enable zoom. 2000+ pixels is recommended.
- **Accepted formats:** JPEG, TIFF, GIF, or PNG. JPEG is preferred for photographs.

**Sneaky detail:** Amazon's systems automatically check background color during image processing. If your white isn't exactly 255,255,255, the system may detect it and flag your listing. It won't always remove it, but it can suppress your image in search results over time.

- **White or light background is strongly recommended** for the main image.

## The Challenge: Products That Don't Cooperate

White backgrounds are easy when your product is a solid, opaque object with clear edges—like a book or a box. But what happens when your product is:

- A glass bottle (transparent, reflective)
- A piece of jewelry (tiny, highly reflective)
- A white shirt on a white background (low contrast)
- A stuffed animal (irregular, fuzzy edges)
- A perfume bottle (glass + liquid + reflection)
- Hair (fine strands that blend into backgrounds)

These products are where traditional background removal fails and where understanding technique matters.

### Transparent Objects

**The problem:** Transparent objects (glass bottles, crystal vases, acrylic items) don't have a solid edge for standard background removal tools to detect. The background shows through the object, making it difficult to separate.

**The solution:**
- Use sidelighting to create visible edges on glass. The light reflects off the edges, defining the shape.
- For product photography, place the glass item slightly away from the white background to create a shadow that defines the shape.
- For post-processing, specialized [BG Remover tools](/bg-remover) trained on transparent objects can handle this. Look for tools that offer "edge refinement" or "transparency handling."
- Use the pen tool or layer masks for manual refinement on tricky transparent products.

### White Products

**The problem:** A white product on a white background creates a "white on white" situation. The edges of the product blend into the background.

**The solution:**
- In photography, use a slight shadow underneath the product to create separation. A subtle drop shadow or a slightly gray surface (that you'll later remove) can help define edges.
- In post-processing, use contrast detection rather than color detection. Look for the edge where the product's white differs from the background's white.
- Add a very subtle shadow in the final image to define the product's shape. Amazon allows "realistic shadows" on main images as long as the background remains pure white.

### Complex Shapes and Hair

**The problem:** Fuzzy edges, hair strands, fur, and feathers are difficult to separate because individual strands are thinner than a pixel.

**The solution:**
- Use a contrasting background during photography (even if you plan to remove it). A bright blue or green screen behind the product makes it easier to detect edges.
- For AI background removal, use tools specifically trained for complex edges. General-purpose tools struggle with hair; dedicated product photo editors handle it better.
- Expect to do manual touch-up on the most complex areas. The AI gets you 90% of the way; the last 10% requires pixel-level work.
- Consider using the "Refine Edge" brush in editing software to clean up hair and fur.

### Reflective and Metallic Objects

**The problem:** Metal and reflective surfaces mirror the environment, including the background. If you remove the background, you may lose the reflections that make the product look metallic.

**The solution:**
- Use a light tent during photography to create soft, even reflections.
- Replace the reflection in post-processing rather than removing it. You want the product to look metallic, so the reflection should appear to come from a white studio environment.
- Use a gradient or subtle curve to simulate the studio reflection after background removal.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Mistake #1: Near-White Backgrounds**
Your background looks white to the human eye, but it's actually RGB 250,250,250. Amazon's detection systems can spot this. Use the eyedropper tool in your editing software to verify the background color is 255,255,255.

**Mistake #2: Harsh Shadows**
A small, soft shadow at the base of the product is acceptable and often looks natural. But harsh, dark shadows or shadows that stretch in one direction violate the "clean background" rule. Use soft lighting during photography to minimize shadows.

**Mistake #3: Jagged Edges**
If you zoom into your image and see pixelated or jagged edges around the product, the background removal was poorly done. These artifacts are visible at full resolution and make your listing look unprofessional. Use feathering or edge refinement to smooth them.

**Mistake #4: Inconsistent Sizing**
Your main product image should have the product filling approximately 85% of the frame. If some images have the product at 50% and others at 95%, your catalog looks inconsistent. Standardize your framing.

**Mistake #5: Ignoring Mobile**
Over 60% of Amazon purchases happen on mobile. On a small screen, a product with a clean white background is much easier to see than one with a busy or dark background. Always check your images at mobile size before publishing.
